# Income Allocation Benchmarks

> **Tax year notice:** This document reflects benchmarks and rules calibrated for tax year 2025.
> Verify social contribution rates and tax brackets against official sources before using for
> a different tax year.

---

## 1. Net Income Denominator

All allocation benchmarks in this document use **net-after-mandatory income** as the denominator — never gross income. Mandatory deductions (social contributions + income tax withheld) vary significantly between Germany and Brazil and must be subtracted before applying any allocation target.

### Germany

**Formula:** Gross − employee social contributions (~19.6%) − income tax (~30-35% effective for typical full-time earner) = **~55-58% of gross**

**Employee social contribution breakdown (2025):**

| Contribution | Employee Share | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Krankenversicherung (KV) | ~7.3% | Allgemeiner Beitragssatz |
| KV Zusatzbeitrag (avg) | ~1.6% | GKV-Spitzenverband 2025 average; individual rates vary by ~0.4-0.8 pp by insurer |
| Rentenversicherung (RV) | 9.3% | |
| Arbeitslosenversicherung (AV) | 1.3% | |
| Pflegeversicherung (PV) | ~1.7% | Childless employees add +0.35% surcharge |
| **Total employee social** | **~19.6%** | Approximate; varies by insurer and family status |

*Source: GKV-Spitzenverband 2025, Deutsche Rentenversicherung, §1 SGB XI.*

> **Note:** KV Zusatzbeitrag varies by insurer. The ~1.6% figure is the GKV-Spitzenverband 2025 published average. Individual employees may pay ~1.2-2.4% depending on their Krankenkasse. For benchmarking purposes, 19.6% is a reasonable approximation; actual net will differ by ±1 pp based on insurer choice.

**Typical gross-to-net conversion (Germany):**

| Gross Income | Effective Income Tax | Social Contributions | Net-After-Mandatory |
|---|---|---|---|
| Up to ~EUR 20,000/year | ~10-15% | ~19.6% | ~65-70% of gross |
| EUR 40,000-70,000/year | ~22-28% | ~19.6% | ~52-58% of gross |
| EUR 70,000-120,000/year | ~30-35% | ~19.6% (capped) | ~46-50% of gross |

*Note: Social contributions are capped above the Beitragsbemessungsgrenze (~EUR 7,550/month in 2025 for RV/AV). At very high incomes the effective social contribution rate as % of gross falls below 19.6%.*

### Brazil

**Formula:** Gross − INSS (7.5-14% capped progressive) − IRRF (22-27.5% effective for typical earner) = **~70-75% of gross**

**INSS progressive table (2025):**

| Gross Monthly Income | INSS Rate |
|---|---|
| Up to R$1,518.00 | 7.5% |
| R$1,518.01 to R$2,594.38 | 9% |
| R$2,594.39 to R$3,856.94 | 12% |
| R$3,856.95 to R$7,786.02 | 14% |
| Above R$7,786.02 | Capped (max contribution at R$7,786.02 bracket) |

*Source: Receita Federal, Portaria MPS 2025. Verify annual adjustment — INSS brackets are adjusted by the government each year.*

> **Note:** INSS is a progressive table — different rates apply to different income bands (not a flat rate on all gross). The maximum INSS contribution is reached at R$7,786.02/month. Effective rate for a typical earner at R$10,000/month gross is approximately 9-11% of gross.

**Typical gross-to-net conversion (Brazil):**

| Gross Monthly Income | Effective INSS | Effective IRRF | Net-After-Mandatory |
|---|---|---|---|
| Up to R$3,000 | ~8% | ~5-10% | ~82-87% of gross |
| R$5,000-R$10,000 | ~10-11% | ~15-20% | ~69-75% of gross |
| R$10,000-R$20,000 | ~9-10% (capped) | ~22-27% | ~63-69% of gross |

## 2. Adjusted 50/30/20 Rule

Applied to **net-after-mandatory income** (D-02). The classic 50/30/20 rule is adapted for DE/BR structural differences — housing costs in German cities are higher relative to net income, and Brazil has mandatory FGTS employer contributions that partially offset voluntary savings requirements.

| Category | DE Range (% of net-after-mandatory) | BR Range (% of net-after-mandatory) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Needs (housing, utilities, food, insurance) | 40-55% | 45-60% | DE: Mietpreise in major cities (Munich, Frankfurt, Hamburg) consume 35-45% of net alone |
| Wants (discretionary, travel, leisure) | 15-25% | 15-25% | Similar in both countries |
| Savings + investments | 20-30% | 15-25% | Voluntary savings + investment contributions |
| Debt repayment | 0-15% | 0-15% | Mortgage, consumer credit, car finance, student loans — separate from needs for clarity |

> **Important:** These ranges are targets, not hard rules. Users in high-cost cities (Munich, Sao Paulo) may structurally exceed the Needs range; the advisor should note the deviation but not treat it as a failure without context.

### Investment Sub-Targets (D-04)

Investment contributions as % of net-after-mandatory income:

**Germany:**
- Minimum: 15% of net-after-mandatory
- Aspirational: 20-25% of net-after-mandatory

**Brazil:**
- Minimum: 10-15% of net-after-mandatory
- Note on FGTS: Mandatory employer FGTS contributions (8% of gross salary) count toward the BR investment rate minimum because FGTS is a forced savings account accessible upon certain life events (home purchase, unemployment, retirement). Do **not** count INSS toward the investment rate — INSS is social insurance, not personal savings.
- Voluntary FGTS extras (FGTS complementar) are optional and should not be assumed unless confirmed in the user profile.

## 3. Emergency Fund Threshold

**Target: 6 months of total monthly expenses**

**Rationale for 6 months (D-03):** Standard guidance in single-jurisdiction employment is 3 months. Cross-border financial lives (DE + BR employment, multi-jurisdiction tax obligations, dual banking systems) carry higher administrative complexity and switching costs. A 6-month buffer provides adequate runway for:
- Job transition across jurisdictions (visa processing timelines, work permit delays)
- Tax filing corrections across two countries
- Currency conversion timing (BRL/EUR volatility)
- Unexpected administrative burdens (German bureaucracy, Receita Federal notices)

**What counts as liquid assets for this purpose:**

| Germany | Brazil |
|---|---|
| Cash (current account, Girokonto) | Cash (conta corrente) |
| Tagesgeld (overnight savings accounts) | Poupanca |
| Festgeld with <= 30-day notice period | CDB de liquidez diaria |
| | LCI/LCA with daily liquidity |

**Does NOT count:** Investment portfolios (ETFs, stocks, FIIs), pension funds (Riester, Rürup, PGBL, VGBL), real estate equity, or any asset that cannot be liquidated within 5 business days without penalty.

## 4. Debt-to-Income Ratio

**Healthy threshold: <= 35% of net-after-mandatory income in recurring debt obligations**

**Includes in this calculation:**
- Mortgage payments (monthly installment, including principal + interest)
- Consumer credit installments (credit card minimum payments, personal loans)
- Car finance
- Student loans

**Excludes from this calculation:**
- Mandatory social contributions (already netted in the denominator — double-counting would be incorrect)
- PGBL/VGBL contributions (voluntary savings, not debt)
- FGTS (employer-side mandatory, not a debt obligation)

> **At 35% threshold:** A user with EUR 3,000 net-after-mandatory income (Germany) should carry no more than EUR 1,050/month in debt obligations. Above this threshold the financial cushion for savings and unexpected expenses becomes structurally constrained.
